# Progress Undone?

Last month, I wrote [a post](https://frostedechoes.com/2022/08/18/the-radiant-citadel.html) on a new Dungeons & Dragons adventure module called *The Radiant Citadel*. The new module has a considered diversity that reflects the realities of humanity. I've read up on the first adventure in the book, and I'm excited to play through it. Not to spoil the ending, but it shows how suspicion between cultures can be overcome through diplomacy (and a bit of sleuthing). In a game that used to be mainly about killing scary monsters and getting to their hoards of treasure, it seems like progress. 

{{more}}

A few days ago, I read a piece from Freddie DeBoer about ethnic and racial representation in popular culture. In [the piece](https://freddiedeboer.substack.com/p/why-are-identitarians-such-cheap), he makes the case that identitarians are incapable of acknowledging progress. 

> Of course, lurking around in the background of this issue lies another of contemporary progressivism’s petty pathologies: the utter inability to acknowledge progress. Because the simple fact of the matter is that movies and TV shows and all manner of other creative industries are now vastly more diverse than they were a decade ago. Great progress has been made. Great and durable progress, it seems to me. And we can’t have that! Few rules in American left-of-center circles are more lustily enforced than the prohibition against acknowledging that things have gotten better. And so all of the vast increase in diversity and representation in the past decade are ignored, the concept of proportionality is not discussed, how we recognize success is avoided, and all of these endlessly-gifed moments and countless breathless headlines are constantly aggregated and yet never allowed to inform our opinion of reality.

As if to prove DeBoer's point, the next day, [a fellow microblogger](https://micro.blog/spgreenhalgh) linked to an [article from Gizmodo](https://gizmodo.com/one-dnd-racism-rpg-stereotypes-dungeons-dragons-wotc-1849531852) by Linda Codega on how racism still plagues Dungeons & Dragons. The article specifically, if barely, touched on *The Radiant Citadel*. 

> More recently, on September 2nd, Wizards of the Coast publicly apologized and removed parts of the new Spelljammer core book after players pointed out offensive racial coding. But some fans were frustrated that the response didn’t identify how the error happened, or lay out any plan to prevent it from happening in the future. Much of the goodwill that had been built up between fans of color and antiracist players after the release of Adventures in the Radiant Citadel (a Dungeons & Dragons anthology primarily created by people of color) was undone. 

Some people perceive the progress of the Radiant Citadel — which has been out all of 2 months now — on matters of race and ethnicity — has been undone. Points have been taken off the scoreboard and the ref has called back the play. The adventure module still exists, though, evidence that D&D is inching in the right direction. Whether that is properly acknowledged doesn't erase the fact that it's out there. 

Of course, there is more to do in D&D regarding reaching goals of racial equity and inclusiveness. For one thing, they could change the use of the word "race" to "species." Tolkien texts aside, orcs don't really resemble men in many ways. It definitely feels wrong to refer to them as a different race. Orcs as player character options with predetermined negative traits seems to be the most often identified problematic aspect of the role playing game. However, even making a change to the language wouldn't satisfy some, as Codega quotes someone acknowledging. 

> And even if WotC did change from the word race to the word species, Isa said, that would still be a problem, “because there would still be a lot of racial coding.”

There are many good people trying to make Dungeons & Dragons more accessible to all kinds of people, but I really wish we could take a minute and admire the beneficial changes that have been made. If they are almost immediately glossed over or diminished, it makes the game's transformation seem impossible. I really do feel bad for people who champion these issues, but can't see themselves as any closer to the kingdom than they were when they started the journey.
