# A Mastodon Frozen In Time

**Mastodon was created in 2016**, and the site still harkens back to its origins, a time when [Gamergate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamergate_(harassment_campaign)) and the atrocious harassment of women in tech on Twitter was still very fresh in our minds. The platform was a reaction to that, and the rising toxic atmosphere on social media. At the time, Twitter was also in the throws of dealing with the side-effects of a Donald Trump candidacy and subsequent presidency. As such, one of the primary features of Mastodon was to stamp out hate and harassment. It's a laudable goal, and very much needed in today's technology landscape. There's a problem there, though, in that Mastodon seems to define itself, at least in part, [as the anti-Twitter](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/11/21/style/mastodon-twitter-adam-davidson.html). 

{{more}}

> But because so much news happens on Twitter — and because Twitter itself is such a news story — the social network symbolized by a tiny bird casts a very large shadow over the social network named after a giant prehistoric beast.

Users of Mastodon spill a lot of digital ink writing about what is commonly referred to there as "the bird site." The overall environment on Mastodon is very meta. It's social media about social media.  

![](_mastodon%20on%20ice.png)

You would be forgiven for thinking that perhaps Mastodon is an overcorrection for the sins of Twitter. Joining most Mastodon instances (servers) is an exercise in immediately learning what types of people and speech they will not permit. Literally, that's the screen that appears first. The prohibitions list usually consists of made-up pathologies you might find in a fake DSM. Ever heard of a _terve_? If I have to look up the meaning of words just to know what is tolerated, I'm not super likely to join. I saw a post from one user stating that anyone who is "gender-critical" is not allowed. I'm not sure what that means, but it feels a little broad. From what I've seen, Mastodon users and moderators seem more interested in policing speech they don't like than in having interesting discussions with diverse viewpoints. It doesn't initially sound like much fun. However, it could be protective if you have been exposed to harassment somewhere else on the internet.

You have to apply for membership in some Mastodon servers. If Steve Rubell lets you behind the velvet rope, then you are in the exclusive club, but mind your p's and q's. Mastodon users seem to spend their time trying to get each other banned. From the article in the NYT quoted above — on journa.host — a user was banned for referring to someone trying to get him banned [as an "activist](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/11/21/style/mastodon-twitter-adam-davidson.html)." The woman who successfully got him banned was shortly thereafter banned herself. It all sounds a bit like an early season of *Survivor* minus the lush tropical surroundings. If the goal is not to get voted off the island, you have to play a very political game. 

## Sensitive Topics

The conventional wisdom used to be that, if you wanted to avoid an argument, you stayed away from politics and religion in your discussions. So it's interesting that most terms of service for Mastodon servers or even a service like omg.lol don't list religion or politics as subjects to avoid. It turns out that you can be as critical as you want about someone's core spiritual or political beliefs, as long as you don't mention their sexual preferences. I've witnessed this playing out in real situations. Even on platforms with stringent community standards, like Micro.blog, I've seen Christians referred to as "demon assholes" and "myth believing cretins." If these types of invectives were leveled at any other group, it would probably lead to a moderation action, either on Micro.blog or Mastodon instances. Put yourself in the place of a believer, and think about how welcome you would feel in an environment with people who hurl such insults at you. Not only that, but the realization that it's tolerated in a way that it wouldn't be for other groups is adding insult to injury. 

In a sense, Mastodon seems frozen in time, a reactionary monument to an ugly moment in internet history. On the other hand, you could easily make the argument that what seems to be the primary mission for Mastodon is still very much needed. If we were concerned about Twitter back in 2016, we've just as much reason to be concerned about the network today. The current CEO seems to want to use it as a proprietary tool to smash his perceived enemies. The was very recently showcased in his special announcement of an inquiry into suppression of [a certain news story](https://www.theverge.com/2022/12/2/23490863/elon-musk-twitter-expose-hunter-biden-flop-doxxed-multiple-people). Under the guise of transparency and building trust, the company's leadership is out to expose the _secret machinations of the bad people_. It feels like a tit for tat that is going to benefit no one.

## Moderation in Moderation

It would be nice to feel like tools of moderation are applied evenly and not geared towards the sensitivities of a particular group of people. I don't think we are going to see this, either on Twitter or on Mastodon. We'll be stuck in these enclaves of political and social resentment, either way. Maybe it's best to just limit our involvement, from a time and mental energy perspective. As Cal Newport [wrote in 2019](https://www.newyorker.com/tech/annals-of-technology/can-indie-social-media-save-us): 

> For the exhausted majority of social-media users, however, the appeal of the proverbial quiet bench might outweigh the lure of a better Facebook.
